Patient Education

1. Daily Dental Care Tips:

Taking care of your teeth every day helps prevent cavities, gum disease, and bad breath.
  1. Brush twice a dayfor two minutes with fluoride toothpaste.
  2. Floss dailyto remove plaque between teeth.
  3. Use mouthwash to reduce bacteria and freshen breath.
  4. Change your toothbrush every 3-4 months (or sooner if the bristles are frayed).
  5. Drink water, especially after meals, to help rinse away food particles.

2. Healthy Eating for a Healthy Smile:

What you eat affects your dental health!


  1. Limit sugary snacks and drinks—they feed the bacteria that cause tooth decay.
  2. Eat crunchy fruits and vegetables like apples and carrots for natural teeth cleaning.
  3. Include calcium-rich foods (milk, cheese, leafy greens) to strengthen your teeth.

3. Common Dental Medications:

Your dentist may prescribe medications to manage dental conditions. Here's what you should know

a. Pain Relievers

  • Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or acetaminophen (Tylenol) can relieve toothache or post-procedure discomfort.
  • Always take as directed and with food, if required.

    • b. Antibiotics

  • Prescribed for dental infections or after procedures like tooth extractions.
  • Finish the full course even if you feel better.
  • Common antibiotics: Amoxicillin, Metronidazole, Clindamycin (if allergic to penicillin).

    • c. Mouth Rinses

  • Chlorhexidine Gluconate: An antibacterial rinse for gum infections.
  • Use as prescribed—usually short term, as long-term use may cause staining.

    • d. Topical Fluoride or Desensitizing Pastes

  • Helps strengthen enamel and reduce sensitivity.
  • Especially useful for patients with early decay or gum recession.

    • 4. Before & After Dental Procedures:

    Before:

  • Inform your dentist of all medications and allergies.
  • Avoid eating before sedation procedures (your dentist will give exact instructions).

    • After:

  • Follow all post-procedure instructions carefully.
  • Take medications on time.
  • soft foods if advised, and avoid using a straw after extractions (to prevent dry socket).

    • 5. When to Call Your Dentist

    1. Persistent pain or swelling after a dental procedure.
    2. Signs of infection (fever, pus, bad taste, severe swelling).
    3. Broken or knocked-out teeth (seek care immediately).

    6. Your Oral Health Affects Your Whole Body

    1. Poor dental health is linked to heart disease, diabetes, and pregnancy complications.
    2. Keeping your mouth healthy helps keep your whole body healthy too!

    Stay on Track with Regular Visits

    Visit your dentist every 6 months for cleanings and checkups—even if you feel fine!
